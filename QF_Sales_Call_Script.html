PREP Using the Sales Brief

Before every call, open the prospect's brief in the dashboard. You don't pitch from it — you listen for it. Pull from the brief only when the prospect hands you the opening.

Load before dialing
  • → Owner's first name + confidence
  • → 1–2 standout review quotes
  • → Top 2 competitor names
  • → Their single biggest site gap (friction angle)
  • → Two job value numbers (e.g. $3k–$8k repiping)
Don't do this
  • ✕ Recite every section in order
  • ✕ Lead with competitor names unprompted
  • ✕ Throw ROI math before they've talked
  • ✕ Read the brief notes verbatim on the call
  • ✕ Skip discovery and go straight to pitch
PHASE 01 Open Get permission. Set the frame.
📌 Your only goal in the open: get 60 seconds of permission. Don't pitch yet. Don't reveal everything. Just get them to agree to hear you out.
If they saw the demo
Warm open — demo drop already sent
+
Hey [Name] — I actually messaged through your website a couple days ago. I found a site that looked like it might be yours and wanted to check if it was connected to [Business Name]. Did someone get a chance to see that link?
💡 If yes, saw it: "Great — that's actually why I'm calling. I built that. I want to talk to you about it. Do you have 60 seconds?"
If no, didn't see it: Treat as cold — send them the link right now and pivot to the cold open script below.
No prior contact
Cold open — calling fresh
+
Hey [Name], my name is [Your Name] — I'm not a customer, I'll be upfront about that. I actually built a website for [Business Name] this week, and I wanted to show it to you before I moved on to someone else in the area. Do you have 60 seconds?
⚠️ Don't say "I'm a web designer" or "I'm calling about your website." Lead with the already-built fact — it's the hook. Their curiosity is your entry point.
Gatekeeper
Getting past staff / receptionist
+
Hey — is [Owner Name / the owner] available? It's [Your Name] — I have something for them, not a sales call.
💡 "I have something for them" is technically true and creates curiosity without being deceptive. If pressed: "I built a website for [Business Name] and want to show it to the owner before I offer it to someone else."
PHASE 02 Discovery Ask questions. Let them talk.
🎙️ This is where most reps fail — they skip straight to the pitch. Don't. The discovery questions are what make the prospect sell themselves. Your job here is to listen and mirror, not talk.
Current situation
Understand where they get business today
+

These questions build the picture. Ask them in a relaxed, curious tone — not an interrogation. One at a time. Let them finish.

  • Q1
    So how does most of your work come in right now — is it mostly referrals, or are you getting people finding you online?
    This tells you if they're referral-dependent or already have some search traffic. Referral-only = they're leaving money on the table and know it.
  • Q2
    And when someone does find you online — what do you think they see first? Google, Yelp, your site?
    Gets them thinking about their own digital presence without you having to tell them it's weak.
  • Q3
    How's business been lately — are you at capacity, or is there room for more work?
    If they're slow, this opens the "you could be filling that gap" angle. If they're busy, pivot to quality over quantity — bigger jobs, not more jobs.
  • Q4
    What kind of jobs do you love doing — the ones you wish you got more of?
    Gold. They'll name the high-ticket jobs (repiping, full installs, boiler replacements). Now you know exactly what the site should be built around.
Current site
Get them to evaluate their own web presence
+
  • Q5
    How do you feel about your current website — does it represent the business the way you'd want a potential customer to see it?
    Most will say "it's fine" or "it's okay, I guess." The hesitation is the tell. Don't jump on it — mirror it back.
  • Q6
    If a homeowner in [neighborhood] searched for [trade] right now — do you think they'd find you?
    Concrete and specific. Forces them to think about the actual search. Most trade owners have no idea how they rank.
Mirror technique
If they say "It's okay I guess, it's a bit outdated" — just repeat the last two or three words back as a question: "A bit outdated?" Then stay silent. They'll fill the silence with more. This is mirroring — it works because it feels like you're listening, not selling.
PHASE 03 Deepen the pain Consequence questions. Let them feel the gap.
🔍 Only go here if discovery revealed a real problem — slow periods, referral dependency, uncertainty about search presence, or frustration with the current site. Don't manufacture pain that isn't there.
Consequence questions
What does the gap actually cost them?
+
  • Q7
    So if a homeowner on [street / neighborhood] is searching right now for someone to handle [the job they said they love] — and they don't see your name — where do you think that call goes?
    Paints the picture of lost revenue without you stating a number. They'll say "probably one of the bigger guys" or name a competitor.
  • Q8
    What's a job like that worth to you — roughly?
    Let them say the number. A $5,000 job means more coming from them than from you. Note it for the ROI close later.
  • Q9
    And how many of those could you realistically take on in a month if they were coming in?
    Now they're doing the math themselves. You don't have to.
then
So just to make sure I'm hearing this right — if nothing changes here, those jobs keep going to someone else. Is that something you're okay with, or is that actually worth fixing?
📌 The question "Is that something you're okay with?" forces a real answer. "No" means you proceed. "Yes" means you probe further — they're hiding something or genuinely aren't the decision maker.
If they say they're busy / don't need more work
Pivot to quality over quantity
+
That's actually great to hear — 25 years in the area, you've built something real. I'm not trying to flood you with calls. What I'm asking is this: are the jobs you're getting right now the ones you actually want? Because the customers in [neighborhood] who have the $1.3 million homes — they're searching online first. That's the market we're talking about. Not more work. Better work.
Reframe technique
Shift from "volume" to "quality of client." Trade owners who are busy often have plenty of small, low-margin jobs and few big ones. The reframe targets their ambition, not their availability.
PHASE 04 The reveal Show them what exists. Don't oversell it.
🎯 The reveal is earned — it lands harder because you just spent 5 minutes understanding their situation. Now you bridge directly from their pain to the solution.
The bridge
Connect what they said to what you built
+
Here's why I'm actually calling. I noticed exactly what you described — [use their friction angle from the brief, e.g., "there was no way to see your services clearly" / "I couldn't find an emergency line" / "the site didn't show off the work you do"]. So I built a version that does. I'm not asking you to pay for anything right now. I just want you to look at it for 30 seconds. Can I text you the link?
💡 Insert the friction angle from their brief — the one specific thing you actually observed on their site (Angle A, B, or C). This makes it feel personal and accurate, not generic.
after they agree to receive the link
Okay I just sent it. Take a look while I've got you — I can walk you through it in 60 seconds if you want, or just tell me what you think when you open it.
Talking points
What to say while they're looking at the site
+

While they're looking, use material from the brief to anchor the value. Only use one or two of these — not all of them.

  • ★
    Your reviews use the word "punctual" more than any plumber I've seen. The site surfaces that front and center — because that's actually what sets you apart in this market.
  • ★
    You've been in [neighborhood] for 25 years. That's longer than most of your competitors have had a web presence here. The site leads with that — because that history is actually a weapon if it shows up correctly.
  • ★
    The page is built around [high-value service they mentioned in discovery] — so when someone searches for that specific job, there's a page for it. Your current site doesn't have that.

Isolate the block
What's keeping them from a 10?
+
Got it — and I appreciate you being straight with me. What specifically do you think is keeping you from a [higher number]? Is it the price, or is there something about the site itself that isn't quite right yet?

Listen to their answer. Then ask one more:

If that were resolved — would this be a yes?
💡 This question is critical. "If that were resolved — would this be a yes?" collapses the objection into a single resolvable condition. If they say yes, you solve the condition. If they keep moving the goalposts, there's a deeper issue — likely price, authority, or they're not the decision maker.
PHASE 06 Close or bridge to video call Two valid wins. Lock in one.
🔀 Read the prospect. If they're high-energy and engaged, push for the close now. If they're analytical or hesitant, the video call is your friend — it's where you share your screen, show them their competitors' rankings, and close with data. Either way, don't leave the call without a commitment.
Path A
Close on the call — send Whop link now
+
Here's how it works — I'll send you a checkout link right now, it takes about two minutes. Once that's done, we handle everything: hosting, domain, going live. You don't touch a thing. Want me to send it over?
if they say yes
Perfect. I'm sending it now — keep an eye on [email / phone]. Once you complete it I'll get confirmation and we'll have everything set up within 24 hours. Any questions before I send?
📌 Send the Whop checkout link immediately. Do not let the call end and then send it — the window closes fast. Stay on with them if possible until they complete it.
Path B
Bridge to video call — close with data
+
I want to be respectful of your time — I think the best thing I can do is jump on a quick video call with you, probably 15–20 minutes. I'll show you exactly where you stand on Google right now, where your competitors are ranking, and what the site does differently. That way you've got the full picture before you decide anything. Are you free [day] or [day] this week?
💡 Give two specific day options — not "when works for you." Open-ended scheduling creates friction and delays. Lock in a time before you hang up.
after they pick a time
Perfect — I'll send you a calendar invite with the video link in the next few minutes. And in the meantime, the demo site link is [URL] if you want to poke around again before we talk. Sound good?
⚠️ Send the calendar invite while they're still on the call if possible, or within 5 minutes of hanging up. A booked call with no invite is a 40% no-show rate. An invite with a link drops that significantly.
OBJECTIONS Handling resistance Every objection is a question in disguise.

Trade owners are skeptical by nature — they get pitched constantly. An objection isn't a no, it's a "convince me." Click each one to see the response.

COMMON "I already have a website." ›
I know — I've seen it. This isn't about replacing it, it's about showing you what the same business looks like with a site built specifically for how customers search in [neighborhood] today. It took me about an hour to put together. I'm not asking you to do anything right now — just look at it for 30 seconds. Want me to text you the link?
📌 Never criticize their current site directly. Frame yours as "what's possible" not "what's wrong with yours." If they push back further: "What specifically do you like about your current site?" — then show how yours has all of that plus more.
COMMON "I get enough work already." ›
That's a good position to be in — seriously. But here's the thing: the work you're getting is mostly referrals, which have a ceiling. I'm talking about a different kind of customer. In [neighborhood], there are homeowners sitting on $1.3 million properties looking for someone to handle [high-value job they mentioned]. That call goes to whoever shows up first on Google. Right now that's not you — and those jobs don't come through word of mouth.
PRICE "How much is it?" ›
Before I get into that — I want to make sure what I built actually makes sense for your business first. Have you had a chance to see the link? … [If yes]: Okay good. One job — one repiping, one boiler replacement — covers this and then some. The price is going to feel like nothing compared to that. Want me to walk you through it?
💡 Never give the price before they've seen the demo. If they haven't seen it, send it first and get them on video before you talk numbers. Price without context always loses.
PRICE "That's too expensive." ›
I hear you. Let me just ask — what's a job like [high-value job they mentioned] worth to you on a good week? … [Let them answer.] Right. So we're talking about one customer — one call that you're not getting right now — covering this completely. That's not really an expense, that's the site paying for itself before the first month is up.
DELAY "I need to think about it." ›
Of course — I wouldn't expect you to decide on the spot. Can I ask though: what specifically do you want to think through? Because if it's something I can clear up right now, I'd rather do that than leave you with an open question.
📌 The goal isn't to pressure them — it's to isolate the real concern. If they name it, handle it. If they can't name it, that's a sign they're not the decision maker or need the video call to feel confident.
DELAY "Call me back next week." ›
No problem at all — I just want to make sure the site is still available when I do. I'm talking to a couple other [trade] businesses in the area and I haven't made any decisions yet. Can we lock in a specific time? I'd rather block it for you than have that conversation after it's gone.
💡 This is true — you do have other leads in the area. The scarcity isn't manufactured. Use it honestly. Book a specific time, not an open "call me back."
AUTHORITY "I'd have to talk to my wife / partner / accountant." ›
Totally understand — and honestly, I'd rather you have the full picture before you make any decision. Can we set up a quick call with the two of you? Even 15 minutes. That way whoever needs to see it, sees it at the same time and you're not playing middleman. What does [partner's] schedule look like this week?
📌 Never ask them to "explain it" to their partner — you lose control of the pitch. Always get the decision maker on a call directly, even if it takes another step.
SKEPTIC "How do I know this will actually get me customers?" ›
That's a fair question and I'm not going to promise you a specific number of leads — nobody can do that honestly. What I can show you is where [Competitor A] and [Competitor B] are ranking for searches in your area right now, and what they've built to get there. The difference between your current site and theirs is exactly the gap we're closing. Want to see that side by side on a quick video call?
TECHNIQUES Core sales methods The psychology behind the script.
Mirroring
Repeat the last 2–3 words they said as a question. Creates the feeling of being heard. Draws out more detail without asking another question. Silence after the mirror is the key — wait for them to fill it.
Labeling
Name what they seem to be feeling: "It sounds like you've had a bad experience with this kind of thing before." Labels defuse resistance. They'll either confirm it (opening the door) or correct it (giving you the real objection).
Consequence questions
Ask what happens if nothing changes. "If you're in the same position six months from now — same site, same referral-only flow — what does that look like?" Gets them to articulate the cost themselves.
Reframing
When they raise price as an objection, reframe against the job value they told you. Never defend the price — make the comparison do the work. "You said a repiping runs $5k. This is less than that."
The 1–10 close
Get a number before you talk price or logistics. A 7 tells you there's a specific objection. A 9 tells you you're close. Anything below 6 means go back to discovery — the pain isn't landed yet.
Two-option close
Never ask an open question at the close. "Are you free Tuesday or Wednesday?" Both options move things forward. Open-ended ("when are you free?") creates friction and delays.
⚠️ The most important rule: The brief is intelligence, not a script. Use it to confirm what you hear from them — not to lead with. If they tell you they love doing boiler replacements, that's when you pull the ROI numbers. If they don't bring it up, you don't either.
